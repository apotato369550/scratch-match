1. Executive Summary

Scratch Match is a locally-bound, AI-assisted employment matching service for the Province of Cebu. The prototype was presented at the CIS 2205 design exhibit, where visitors interacted with the landing page, persona handouts, tri-fold brochure, standee, and a live walkthrough of the seeker, employer, and administrator portals. A short Google Form collected scored ratings (1–5) on four dimensions — usability, design, functionality, and content clarity — and free-text suggestions for each.

Across 27 responses gathered between 11 May and 28 May 2026, the prototype averaged 4.70 / 5.00 on every scored dimension. Open-text comments most often praised the minimalist visual approach and the clarity of the on-screen instructions. The single most consistent forward-looking request was a mobile version of the application, mentioned by six separate respondents. The most consequential single response surfaced a cluster of working-state defects — a failed sign-in attempt, a missing CV upload entry point on the seeker dashboard, and newly posted jobs not appearing in the employer listing — that the team had not yet caught in internal testing.

The primary goal of this document is therefore twofold: to organise the raw feedback against the categories required by the course rubric, and to translate the resulting themes into a concrete refinement plan that distinguishes which elements of the "as-is" prototype should be retained, revised, or added in the "to-be" version.

Interpretation

The ranking above is sorted strictly by mention count, but the team's response cannot follow the same order. The top three themes — mobile access, UI polish, and content brevity — are refinements; the prototype works, and visitors are asking for a better surface on top of it. The fourth-ranked theme, by contrast, is the only one that questions whether parts of the system work at all. A single respondent, who otherwise rated design 5/5, walked through three concrete reproduction steps that the team had not caught internally. Frequency alone would put this fourth; severity pulls it forward in the action plan in Section 6.

Positive highlights were broad rather than thematic. Phrases such as "very straightforward", "clear instructions", "well used", and "great UI" appeared across responses on every scored dimension. The one written outlier — "bland" — is the inverse of the same minimalism that most respondents praised, which is consistent with the design intent and does not by itself justify a rework.

5. Evaluation Against Original Design

The original design brief committed to a three-role landing surface (job seeker, employer, administrator), a local-only data story, and a deliberately restrained visual language. The exhibit feedback is mapped back to those design choices below.

  • Navigation: the three-role split and the "Choose your role" section read as obvious to visitors; no respondent reported being unsure where to click first. The one correction is the administrator route, which felt unexpectedly reachable and should be hidden from the public landing surface even though it is already gated server-side.
  • Readability / Typography: no complaints about font size, line length, or contrast were submitted. The Playfair-and-Inter pairing inherited from the design system can be retained as-is for the next prototype.
  • Instructional Clarity: the most direct praise was for the on-screen instructions, which respondents described as "clear" and "straightforward". The two improvements raised were a request for shorter copy in some places and a specific request for asterisks on required form fields.
  • Responsiveness: not directly tested in the booth (the demo was driven on a laptop), but six respondents asked for a mobile experience. This is the single largest gap between the original brief, which targeted "single-machine deploy" without explicitly addressing form-factor, and what visitors expected from a public-facing service.
  • Accessibility: one general ask. The original brief did not enumerate accessibility targets, so this is treated as an additive requirement rather than a regression.

6.2 Final Refinement Summary

  • Elements to Retain: the minimalist navy-and-gold visual language, the three-role landing structure, the persona-led storytelling on the brochure and standee, and the local-only / on-premise data story that drew positive comment during walkthroughs.
  • Elements to Revise: the sign-in → first-feature happy path so that a stranger can complete it end to end on a fresh database; the seeker dashboard so the CV upload control is visible at first glance; the employer dashboard so newly posted jobs reliably appear in the list; and the public landing so the administrator route is no longer suggested to non-admin visitors.
  • New Ideas to Implement: a responsive layout pass for mobile browsers, asterisks on required form fields, and a structured accessibility audit covering contrast, focus states, and keyboard navigation.

7. Conclusion

The exhibit confirmed the central premise of Scratch Match. Visitors understood the pitch without lengthy explanation, rated all four scored dimensions at 4.70 / 5.00 on average, and singled out the clarity of the on-screen instructions as the prototype's strongest quality. The most common forward-looking request was a mobile version, which is a refinement of reach rather than a critique of concept.

The single critical review did not dispute the idea. It surfaced gaps between the path the team rehearsed in the demo and the rest of the feature surface — sign-in, CV upload, employer job listing, and the visibility of the admin route — that are addressable without changing the architecture. Treating that one detailed review as a high-severity input, rather than averaging it out against the majority of high scores, is the most important judgement this document records.

The "to-be" prototype is therefore not a redesign. It keeps the same pitch, the same three-role shape, and the same restrained visual language that visitors responded to. What changes is reliability and reach: every primary path must work end to end on a fresh database, the layout must answer a phone-sized screen, and the small but real asks — required-field markers, tighter copy, accessibility basics — should land in the same iteration so that the next time a stranger sits at the keyboard, the prototype tells a complete story rather than a rehearsed one.
